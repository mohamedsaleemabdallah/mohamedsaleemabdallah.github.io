Ceylon Tea Musuem

The Ceylon Tea Museum is housed in a building that was once the Hatane Tea Factory which was first built in 1925. The tea factory was abandoned for more than a decade before the Chairman of the Tea Board of Sri Lanka, Clifford Ratwatte, incorporated it into the Ceylon Tea Museum in 1998. The Sri Lanka Tea Board and Planters Association ensured that the building was used for its new Refurbished designation as a museum and officially opened to the public on December 1st, 2001.


The Ceylon Tea Museum in Hantane is three kilometers from the city of Kandy and has motorable road surrounding the museum, providing easy access and ample parking for cars and coaches. The museum consists of four floors. The ground floor and second floor display old tea processing machines such as generators, rollers, dryers, fermenting tables and sorting machines from the 19th century and the first floor consists of a library and an Auditorium with facilities for audiovisual presentations. The third floor is dedicated to the tea shops where a selection of fine Sri Lankan teas are available. The entire top floor is scenic cafe where guests can enjoy a complimentary cup of tea after the tour.


A panoramic view of the city of Kandy surrounded by the beautiful hills of Hunasgiriya, Knuckles Range and Matale can be viewed through a fourth floor telescope. The area around the tea museum is decorated with different types of tea. Kandy is a must-see on virtually any tourist itinerary, and the location of the Ceylon Tea Museum in Hantane enhances the mountainous region's visitor appeal. Hantane's proximity to the Peradeniya Botanical Gardens and Loolecondera Estate, where tea was first commercially grown in Sri Lanka, makes it the perfect location.
